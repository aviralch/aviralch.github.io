TLDR : It was because of a pull request

Initially, I wanted to be a software engineer because I used to play games and thought it would be pretty cool as everyone suggested doing what you like. Later on, I realized playing and developing are completely different skills. Still, I kept on going and taking technical classes, coding projects because I thought well it is still a decent gig (it pays well and everyone seems to be rushing to become one).

That all changed eventually when I landed my first internship at a startup and I have never felt so out of place in my life when I felt it then. I was surrounded by so many brilliant people who were constantly solving, innovating, designing, and juggling so many hats so perfectly.

The first week I thought this was not for me, but I carried on since I did not know what else to do. The second week, I forced myself to stare at the dual monitors and understand the codebase because again I did not know what to do, so I did what was expected of me. The third week, I started working on a ticket and made a pull request to add in my changes. The fourth week I found someone using the feature I had added, and it was then it clicked I had written this piece of code that was being used by other people. I then realized that this is something worth doing. The fifth week and onwards I realized I was no longer coding because it was a ‘decent gig’.

Rather I was coding or better put, I was solving and answering questions that would enable and help other people in life. The spirit of my code changed from just answering the question to understanding what it is that is need, why is it needed, and more importantly how to best approach the answer rather than just spitting it out in some half hazardous way making the life of everyone ever to use your code a living help.

It’s not just about arriving at the answer, it’s about knowing and realizing how your answer impacts others. This led to me diving into discovering and learning a lot more skills and languages. I even went onto teaching it to teach it to my peers at the University of Maryland, in a student-led course about UI/UX.
